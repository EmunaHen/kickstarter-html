Physics for Makers: A Hands-On Homeschool Curriculum with 16 STEM Crates

Physics for Makers is a year-long, hands-on homeschool curriculum for middle and high school students — built by makers, for aspiring makers. Each unit pairs engaging, real-world physics with 2 physical STEM project that kids actually build and use for experiments and learning activities. 

That's right, this course is loaded with STEM kits in the curriculum, and is written for OLDER kids, because wanting to have fun while learning doesn't just stop at age 12.

I love Physics, Engineering, Science, Building, Making, etc.  My knowledge of these fields lets me make some pretty cool, fun, and useful stuff.  I want to pass this knowledge along to the next generation.

I also believe that most traditional science / math classes are actually pretty bad at teaching useful knowledge and skills, and they are definitely bad at being fun (at least for most kids).  Why?  I think that they are taught a bit backwards.  They dump facts and procedures on students, until they can't help but wonder:

  • Why do I have to learn this?
  • When will I actually use this?

I prefer more of a "Project Based Learning" style of teaching. What does that mean?
Basically this:

Step 1: Give someone a task that they actually want to do and problems that they actually want to solve.

Step 2: They will seek out the knowledge and skills necessary to complete those tasks and solve those problems.  

This makes motivation more natural and makes learning stick, because it's tied to physical, emotional experiences in your memory, and makes it so that the knowledge and skills gained are actually applicable to getting better at the things that you want to be good at!

I don't believe in a 100% Project-Based Learning model either, which is why my course features a hybrid approach.  You can learn more about my teaching philosophies by watching this Episode of the OpenEd Podcast where they had me as a guest. 
(Sorry, I know it's long. But we are talking about your – or more likely your kid's – education, and I am asking for a large chunk of change, so it's probably worth the time to research if my style is the right fit for you.)

Here's another example of learning with a project and then gaining some new knowledge after:
  
In this example, a couple of young kids participated in an online bridge-building competition that I held.  After I received the video submissions, I made my own video where I picked a couple of them to talk about and teach some good engineering principles. 
(I'm sorry about the poor video quality. I have gotten better equipment since this video was made, and my editing skills are also rapidly improving.)

I actually showed that video to my 10-year-old nephew Shane, who is also one of the kids in the video.  Afterwards, I quizzed him on the stuff that I had taught in the video.
  
The result? 

He fully understood ALL of the concepts that I had explained in the video, even the Moment of Inertia stuff (which is a college-level engineering concept)!  How was this average 10-year old able to grasp college level concepts? 

  • Was it because I did such an amazing job at explaining it? 
    - I wish I could say that I'm just that good, but that's not really the case.
     
  • Was it because the video was super engaging with top-tier production value? 
    - Nope, no. Definitely not.
  • Was it because Shane is some kind of genius, wiz kid? 
    - He's bright, but.... no.
     
  • Was it because he was worried about retaining the knowledge so that he could get a good grade? 
    - No. This wasn't even school-related and he had no idea that I was going to quiz him afterwards.
     
  • It was because he was invested and interested in the whole video because he had a personal emotional experience to tie it to and that experience brought out a desire to try to get better at bridge-building.

That's what we are doing here: Blending tangible experiences with the theoretical concepts so that those concepts make sense and stick in your brain.

What's in the Crates?

Each crate includes:

  • Tools and parts for a hands-on project
  • A mini lesson plan linked to core physics concepts
  • QR code to video tutorials and challenges

The Crates go hand-in hand with the curriculum for a comprehensive learning experience.

  • One full year of instruction (organized into 8 units with 2 Crates each)
  • Interactive online textbook (videos, text, animations)
  • Printable or optional printed workbook
  • Real-world project guides and physics challenges with video instruction
  • Simple math explanations that support the concepts (not the other way around)

Each crate goes through a development cycle as shown below.  It involves several trips around the cycle visiting the following steps each time:

  •  Design: Usually on the Computer.  I create both 3D and 2D CAD files for each custom component of the design, and research to find the best options for off-the-shelf components.
     
  • Prototype:  I create a physical version of the design.  I make all of the protypes myself, often using my Laser Cutters, 3D Printer, and a variety of hand tools.  This allows me to crank out prototypes much faster and cheaper than many designers which allows me to do a lot of design iterations (trips around the design cycle) so that I can identify and fix all of the problems and finish with a better overall product.
     
  • Test: We do 2 phases of testing:
    • In-house Testing (to make sure it works) 
    • Beta Testing (to make Sure that kids can use it)
  • In-House Testing: I test the crates myself to make sure that they function properly.  I also get some friends or family to test it a bit to make sure that someone besides me can also get it to function properly. 
    This phase is typically done quite literally in my house.
     
  • Beta Testing: I send crates and instructional materials out to kids to see if:
    •  those kids and parents can figure out how to properly use the crate without my help, 
    • if they find new ways to break it that I hadn't anticipated, and 
    • if the kids enjoy it.Some crates involve just a few trips around the cycle for me to get it right. Others involve many more.  

      The Bottle Blaster shown in the graphic above took about 50 iterations or trips around the cycle (which is a lot more than most).  

      Why did it take so many trips around the cycle, you ask? How could this process matter enough to do 50 times?
      In the words of Elyse Myers, "Great question. I'd love to tell you."

      The product is a bottle rocket that is safe and easy enough for even kids as young as 5 years old to be able to use themselves in their own backyard, and fun enough for teenagers and adults to enjoy.  

      I experimented with different 3D-printing materials to make the nozzles from, as well as other fabrication methods to make the nozzles -- like epoxy molding in silicone molds, and injection molding.  
      I tried small changes in the shape and size of the plastic parts. 
      Different sizes and materials for the rubber gaskets.  

      After a while, it worked great for me, but it was still too hard for kids to use themselves.

      So I overhauled the whole design to incorporate a gun-style hose attachment that made it 1000% easier for kids to both attach and release their rockets.  

      Finally, after a few more tweaks and iterations, and lots of beta testing to get that all perfect, I arrived at the final design (See video below. Seriously, watch it. I put in a lot of work to birth this kit).

Risks and challenges

The Main Challenge: Writing the textbook, workbook, and accompanying videos and activities will be the biggest challenge. I have never been a classroom teacher and I don't particularly enjoy writing. I do, however enjoy sharing my knowledge, and this is a subject that I am very knowledgeable and passionate about. I've never written an academic course before, but I do know Physics quite well and I know Fun even better. Not only am I capable of combining Physics and Fun, I don't actually think that I can stop myself from combining those 2 things. My biggest challenge would be pacing myself over a long period of time to develop everything that I need to for an entire year's worth of written material, videos, and kits. My Plan to Overcome This: I am actually going to be teaching this same curriculum in-person to 3 high-school students at a local homeschooling Co-Op and to a small group of online-only beta testers. I will be writing the curriculum as I go so that I can stay ahead of the students. This will give me the structure and motivation that I need to keep writing/designing/filming/editing at a steady pace while focusing on the essential parts of the course and not getting distracted by unnecessary "side-quests". Other Challenges (that aren't very big challenges for us because we already have a decade of experience with aspects of creating and delivering cool products) : - Designing, Testing, + Improving the Kits: I am a mechanical engineer with a talent for innovative product design, so this is totally in my wheelhouse. I will manufacture almost all of the custom pieces using one of my 3 laser cutters, my 3D printer, or any of the other tools + machines that I have within a 50 ft radius of where I am writing this right now. Doing it in-house allows me to prototype, test, re-design, and iterate very quickly. It also means that the exact same methods will be used to make the finished crates as the prototype crates. Once again, we will be doing most of the manufacturing, assembling, boxing, and shipping in-house so we can guarantee doing a good job (most of the time - we are human) and we will not be at the mercy of a 3rd party doing their portion of the process whenever they feel like getting around to it. - Quality Control + Support: We will do our best to not have any issues here, but if they do arrive faulty, you will be able to contact us DIRECLY for help, so you won't be dealing with a foreign call center or a clueless, underpaid employee. Environmental commitments

Long-lasting design

Incorporating Modular Design. The physical components should rarely break or wear-out, but if they do, it should be easy to replace the faulty piece rather than replacing the whole thing. Many of the parts will be generic, off the shelf things - Motors, switches, rubber bands, paper clips, etc. that can be easily replaced and even upgraded in may cases. The kits are made to be assembled by the users without glue, so they can also be easily disassembled for long-term storage or for repair.

Reusability and recyclability

While many of the components will not be particularly recyclable, the physical kits will be very reusable and re-purpose-able. The kits can all be dissembled and stored in their original packaging to be pulled out later and used again with another sibling. They will very rarely include anything that will get dried up, used up, or otherwise made unusable over time.

Sustainable materials

The Crates will be made almost entirely from wood from ethically farmed lumber. All kits are designed to minimize material waste when they are cut from the sheet materials.

Sustainable Distribution

I will ship using a pick-up service and by shipping as many items together as I can. I ship over 50% of my products using re-used packaging material (boxes, bubble mailers, packing paper, etc.) rather than using exclusively new materials. When I have to use new materials, I always make sure that they are as minimal as possible and in many cases, I make my own boxes from reclaimed carboard scraps. I use online instructions rather than hard-copy instructions.
