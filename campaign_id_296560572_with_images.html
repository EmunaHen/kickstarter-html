Story

Hello everyone, I have over ten years of experience in intelligent hardware research, development, and manufacturing. Leveraging this valuable expertise, I have developed an open-source smart hardware device designed to help users get started quickly and bring their own products to market efficiently.

To meet the demands of diverse application scenarios, I have integrated a variety of sensors into the device, including temperature, humidity, atmospheric pressure, ultrasonic, PIR, camera, and thermal imaging sensors. Additionally, the device features standard I2C and SPI interfaces, enabling it to connect with more than 20,000 different types of external sensors, offering strong expandability. 

In terms of communication capabilities, I have equipped the device with multiple connectivity modules such as 4G LTE, Wi-Fi, Ethernet, BLE Bluetooth Low Energy, and LoRa, ensuring flexible adaptation to various network environments and stable, reliable cloud connectivity.

Considering different power supply scenarios, the device supports  adapter power, lithium battery power, or a dual-power mode combining both, ensuring stable operation in any environment.

Most importantly, all firmware code for the device is open-sourced and hosted at github.com/aiotfactory, making it easy for developers to learn, modify, and perform further development.

Alongside the hardware, I have also developed a powerful cloud management platform, the full source code of which is also available on GitHub at github.com/aiotfactory. This platform offers comprehensive features such as user management, device management, module management, module viewing, online IDE code editing and compilation, and OTA remote firmware updates. It also provides rich APIs for seamless integration with third-party systems.

To empower the device with advanced intelligent analysis capabilities, my cloud platform has been integrated with a Large Language Model (LLM). This allows sensor data to be processed and analyzed intelligently — for example, detecting falls, identifying object drops, or counting foot traffic — truly bridging the gap from “sensing” to “cognition.”

Lastly, once customers have validated their product concepts, I can offer customized R&D and manufacturing services to fully support mass production and product commercialization.

Risks and challenges

After more than a year of research, development, and testing, I have completed the experimental production of 50 units. Long-term performance tests have shown excellent stability. Therefore, from a technical standpoint, the risk of entering mass production is relatively low. However, the following potential risks may still arise during the process: Risk of Discontinued Components Although I have selected components with strong universality and stable supply chains during the design phase, there is still a possibility that certain parts may be discontinued due to supplier strategy changes or market fluctuations, which could affect future production. Mitigation Strategy: I will continuously monitor project progress and supply chain dynamics, proactively forecasting and purchasing critical components in advance. At the same time, I will prepare alternative designs and compatibility plans in advance to ensure rapid switching without compromising product performance. Risk of Component Price Increases If the prices of core chips or sensors rise significantly beyond our current budget, it may impact the overall profitability of the product. Mitigation Strategy: For key materials prone to price fluctuations, I will assess risk levels based on market trends and make strategic pre-orders or bulk purchases at appropriate times to lock in costs. Additionally, I will explore more cost-effective alternatives without compromising performance.

Long-lasting design

Modular architecture: Design the product using interchangeable modules that can be upgraded or replaced individually (e.g., a motherboard or sensor module that can be swapped without replacing the whole device). Repairability: Provide clear documentation, schematics, and access to replacement parts. Make it easy for users to fix their own devices. Future-proofing: Choose widely supported interfaces and standards (like USB-C or open communication protocols) to ensure compatibility over time. Warranty and support: Offer long-term software/firmware updates and technical support to extend the useful life of the product.

Reusability and recyclability

Standardized components: Use common, off-the-shelf parts that can be reused in other DIY or maker projects. End-of-life instructions: Include clear guidance on how to disassemble and recycle different parts of the product. Take-back program: Encourage users to return old units for proper recycling or refurbishment. Open documentation: Make schematics, PCB layouts, and mechanical drawings available so others can repurpose or modify your design.

Sustainable materials

Biodegradable or recycled plastics: Consider using PLA (polylactic acid), recycled ABS, or other sustainable polymers for enclosures or cases. Metal alternatives: Where possible, use aluminum or other metals that have high recyclability rates. Low-carbon footprint materials: Source materials locally when possible to reduce transportation emissions. Minimalist packaging: Use FSC-certified cardboard, biodegradable fillers, and avoid unnecessary plastic in packaging.

Sustainable Distribution

Localized manufacturing or fulfillment hubs: Set up regional production or assembly centers to reduce shipping distances. Consolidated shipments: Encourage backers to order all items together rather than multiple small shipments.
