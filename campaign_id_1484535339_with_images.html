Story

OmniTrek: The Future of Movement in VR Starts Here

When I first strapped on a VR headset, I was blown away. The visuals were sharp, the tracking was smooth, and for a moment — I really felt like I was there.

But then I tried to move.

Suddenly, the illusion broke. I was teleporting awkwardly, dealing with motion sickness, or walking into furniture. The headset had evolved — but the way we move in VR? It hadn’t.

That moment sparked what would become a five-year journey: to build the first truly immersive, affordable, and intuitive omnidirectional treadmill for VR. One that makes moving through digital worlds feel as natural as walking through your living room.

Why OmniTrek Is Different

Most VR treadmills on the market either cost as much as a high end car or are cheap, feel clunky and unnatural to use.

OmniTrek changes that.

We’ve designed a system that delivers high-end performance without the high-end price tag. The current prototype is already running — and most importantly, it feels right. It’s responsive, smooth, and lets users walk freely in any direction while staying safely on the platform.

This isn’t just a idea — it’s a working proof-of-concept that overcomes the biggest technical hurdles I encountered when starting this project more than five years ago.

Five Years of Building, Breaking, and Building Again

OmniTrek didn’t appear overnight. It’s the result of years of testing, learning, and relentless iteration. From early napkin sketches and cardboard mockups to welding frames and wiring up sensors, every step was a step forward. Some builds failed completely — but each failure taught me something critical. Over time, I cleared major technical hurdles that seemed impossible at first, and the prototype you see today is the direct result of those long nights in the garage and lab.

Risks and challenges

Challenges We’re Ready to Tackle We’re working to fine-tune motion accuracy and reduce latency to make movement feel natural and responsive in VR. Long-term durability is important to us, and we’re exploring ways to make the design hold up well with regular use. Reducing noise and vibration is part of our ongoing development to make the treadmill comfortable in any setting. We take safety seriously and are designing the experience to be as user-friendly and stable as possible—especially for new users. Making the treadmill work across different VR systems may be complex, but it’s a challenge we’re confident we can meet. Coordinating the mechanical and software systems takes work, but we believe in our ability to bring it all together. Moving from prototype to full production will require adjustments, but we’re approaching it step by step. We expect to go through multiple rounds of testing and improvement, and we see that as a necessary and worthwhile part of building something great.
