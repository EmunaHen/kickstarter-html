Story

The Context

The past few months have been challenging for citizen privacy, with messaging apps facing increasing pressure to provide access to private messages.
Fortunately, some companies resisted, but for how long?

Privacy is a fundamental human right that protects individuals from surveillance, manipulation, and exploitation. It ensures that people can express themselves freely, without fear of being monitored or judged. In the digital age, where personal information is constantly being collected, stored, and analyzed, safeguarding privacy is crucial to maintaining autonomy and security. Without privacy, individuals lose control over their personal data, and the risks of misuse, identity theft, and even social or political repression grow significantly. Privacy is not just about hiding information, it’s about protecting freedom and dignity in an increasingly interconnected world.

In the world we are currently living, digital privacy is very rare, and becoming rarer. Most messaging apps, even so-called "secure" ones, rely on centralized infrastructure that makes them vulnerable to breaches, censorship, and hidden compromises.

That’s why I created Shushr, a secure messaging app built on a radical principle: 
no servers, no gatekeepers, and no compromises. 


I'm Ludovic, a software developer and ethical hacker with over a decade in the cybersecurity world. Shushr uses decentralized encryption and asynchronous key exchange to make your messages invisible, even to us.

If you believe privacy shouldn't be optional, participate and join the journey !

The Solution

From that realization, I decided to create something truly inviolable, based on two main principles:

  • Asynchronous key decentralization:
    A major flaw of existing messaging apps is that all encryption keys are ultimately stored on servers. Even if those keys are encrypted and protected, they remain accessible to someone with enough privileges.
  • Data storage decentralization:
    When you know where to look, it's much easier to find what you want — keys, messages, files, and so on. Decentralizing storage adds a critical layer of protection.

While regular messaging apps rely on centralized architectures, we at Shushr aim to provide something far more resilient to real-world attack scenarii.

By doing so, we mitigate two of the three major attacks.

The Challenges

Of course, building such a solution is not easy.
Because we can't rely on "lazy" servers to execute tasks for us, every device must become intelligent.

Each device must independently handle:

  • Message processing logic
  • Key exchange and import
  • Communication with the notification servers
  • And more...

The Target

This project is built with a variety of people in mind, all of whom share a common need for secure, private communication:

  • Citizens who want their privacy respected, free from the constant worry of data breaches or their personal information being exposed.
  • Companies looking for a communication system that remains unbreakable, even in the face of severe cyber incidents, such as a complete compromise of their information systems.
  • Governments seeking a safe, encrypted way to communicate sensitive information, ensuring their messages stay secure in an increasingly uncertain digital world.
  • Journalists who need a trusted platform to protect their sources and ensure sensitive information never leaks, keeping their work and integrity safe.

We believe everyone deserves a platform where privacy is not just an option, but a given.

The Project 

We have already built some proof-of-concept applications — and they work well!
Through this early work, we were able to identify many of the key challenges ahead.

But a working prototype isn't enough.
Before we can move to a production-grade version, we need to go even further, including:

  • Massive message sending tests (Gatling-style load testing)
  • Support for Apple devices (currently tested only on Android)
  • Exploration of new attack scenarios — and defense strategies
  • Beta tests, see how it goes with many users at a time
  • Improvements to the app's workflow (UI/UX)
  • Legal challenges, the hardest ones
  • Self-hosted servers (Docker containers)

It's a step toward meaningful technological sovereignty.
