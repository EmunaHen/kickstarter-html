Story

Introducing our brand-new mini Polyfloss machine — a compact, powerful tool that transforms plastic waste into soft, versatile fibers, using a process inspired by cotton candy. These fibers can be used in a wide range of applications: textiles, packaging, DIY projects, branding materials, interior design, and even construction.

Whether you’re a maker, textile artist, teacher, designer or DIY enthusiast, the Polyfloss opens up a world of local recycling possibilities — right where you are.

You’re part of a creative textile community?

Maybe you’re in a textile school, craft collective, or design studio. You already know how to spin, weave, knit, or braid — now imagine doing all that with plastic fibers made from your own local waste! With the Polyfloss machine, you can recycle shampoo and water bottles, food and medecine packaging for example — and turn them into usable threads or stuffing for your projects. 

Waste becomes a resource - unleash your craft.

You’re a maker or part of a FabLab?

Every FabLab has that drawer full of failed 3D prints and leftover plastic scraps, right? With Polyfloss, you can recycle them into soft fibers, perfect for packaging fragile objects or fill cushions, sleeping bags or your beloved down jacket. But maybe most importantly: you can iron the fibers and create sheets, which can then serve in your laser cutter to create merch, logos, engravings or any creative project. No more buying expensive plastic sheets for your FabLab community!

Already into plastic recycling?

In that case, the Polyfloss is the perfect add-on. It lets you recycle plastics that are often ignored (like PET or PETG) and expand your range of materials and techniques. But, even better, it can match your existing equipment to create new products! Build insulating panels by pressing the fibers in your panel sheet machine, or use an oven to create lightweight and insulating bricks, boxes or products — pressed Polyfloss becomes a great alternative to Expanded Polystyrene (EPS), one of the most polluting plastics. 

Polyfloss doesn’t replace your tools — it multiplies what you can do with them.

How does it work?

 The plastic arrives hot, under pressure, in the head, with a controllable pace. The spinning head has a precision slit under its perimeter, and the plastic is spun by centrifugation into thin fibers. The fibers, just created, are blown with cold air to first elongate them and also cool them for collection. They can then be collected on a grid, in a bag or with a rotational collector to directly create yarns. 

Each plastic needs its own set of temperatures and rotational speeds, as they each have different melting points and viscosities. But the control of the machine has been made super simple with a full in-house developed software and a tactile tablet to pre-program everything. Once the right parameters are set, you just have to press play!

The Plastic issue

We all know it — plastic pollution is one of the biggest environmental crises of our time. Not 10% of plastic waste is recycled globally, while the production of virgin plastic keeps increasing every year. Hundreds of millions of tons of plastic are still ending up in our rivers, oceans, and soil, threatening entire ecosystems and communities. Meanwhile, large-scale industrial recycling can’t keep up — and often ignores small or mixed plastics. It’s time to act differently. With the Polyfloss machine, you can take action right where you are, turning local waste into valuable materials and helping build small-scale circular economies in your own neighborhood.

Why cotton-candy? 

Our machine turns plastic into fibers — but why fibers? While it’s already possible to make bricks, sheets, or panels out of recycled plastic on a small scale (see our friends fromMAD Plastics!), panels or bricks are already widely available with other materials like wood, ceramics or metal. Fiber, on the other hand, is where plastic can truly shine. By spinning waste plastic into fibers, we unlock new ways to reuse plastic through familiar techniques — such as: textile crafts, molding and shaping to replace Expanded Polystyrene applications or even ironing to create flexible sheets. 

This opens up creative, low-tech, and accessible recycling paths that anyone can use — whether you’re a crafter, a designer, a student or a maker.

Makers, inventors, entrepreneurs for change

We are a collective of designers, engineers, entrepreneurs and researchers working at the intersection of ecology, local empowerment, and innovation.

The founding team initially met at Imperial College London and Royal College of Art in London, at theInnovation Design Engineering program back in 2011. We had this idea to create foam out of plastic waste there, and thought of candyfloss. Between 2012 and 2018, Polyfloss was mostly an idea concretized in prototypes: we were doing workshops, exhibitions and talks.

In 2019, everything changed. We partnered with several NGOs who were looking for ways to recycle plastics in humanitarian settings. The idea was getting real, actually helping people where it mattered most. We developed new machines, and installed our first pilots in Gaziantep, near the Syrian border (see below for more infos). We then continued with projects in Nepal, France, Norway and are about to go to Sudan for the UN. 

Building on that, we created our company in 2022, and started growing the team. Karl joined us as an apprentice in engineering and maintenance, Carla on UX/UI and graphic design, Fred on production management and engineering, Elisa on sales and marketing, and recently Zouhair for community management and social networks. 

Focus on Humanitarian aid and development initiatives

Production details 

Our machine is ready to be produced! The main technological component (the rotating head) has been extensively used for the last 2 years and has been improved to withstand long production runs. We just have a few updates finalize: on the software, improve the program and add some features of data logging and maintenance capacities. On the hardware, add some features for the certification requirements. This should take about 2 months of work. 

We have secured our supply chain, with most of the parts being produced or supplied from Europe. The precise machining of the stainless steel and bronze elements are done by a French company, specialized in aeronautics and Formula 1 fabrication! Electronics are all off-the-shelf international suppliers, sheetmetal and 3D Prints are done in Italy. Only the extrusion screw and motors are ordered directly from China. 

Lead time

Our lead times mostly depend on the certification process, which is mandatory for exporting in most countries, like the US, Brazil or China. This process, done by an independent body, can take up to 6 months. In parallel, we'll be ordering all the main parts of the machine to our suppliers, leaving time for assembly, quality tests and packing. We'll also produce all the support videos, setup and maintenance guides.

Risks and challenges

On the technology side, the last big step we have to overcome is certification. Although we have already done it for other technologies in the past, this certification might lead to minor changes in the technology and parts, which might lead to additional production costs. We'll make sure to keep a "design to cost" approach and decide on the most relevant changes when needed. Another challenge we face relates to current international business instability. Like all businesses working in hardware today, the fluctuating taxes and tariffs adds risks on the final prices and capacities to deliver. This is why we haven't included import taxes and delivery prices in the kickstarter, as it would have been too risky to bet on those over the next 6 months. We truly hope that those tariffs between the EU, the US, China and the rest of the world will have settled by then.
